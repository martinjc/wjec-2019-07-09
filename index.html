Bridging the Skills Gap: Innovation in Journalism Education


Martin J Chorley & Glyn Mottershead


2019-07-09 - WJEC

Hello, and thank you. I'm Dr Martin Chorley, I'm from the School of Computer Science and Informatics at Cardiff University. I'm not a journalist, and up until a few years ago would not have considered myself anything like a journalism educator, so I'm very pleased to be here at WJEC.
A little over 7 years ago, I was contacted out of the blue over Twitter by my colleague Glyn Mottershead, who at that point I had never met or spoken to. He wanted to discuss an idea with me, and so because meeting strangers off the Internet is the sort of thing I do, we met and we talked - a wide ranging discussion covering all sorts of geeky topics, including the overlap between Computer Science and Journalism. The end result of this conversation was that we ended up convincing ourselves that we needed to design a new MSc programme to cover what we understood as a skills gap in Journalism education. 2 years of university approval process and a lot of paperwork later, we were able to launch what is now known as the MSc in Computational and Data Jouralism. Designed with a strong input from industry, the course focuses on practical skills and aims to deliver graduates with the technical skills needed for a range of roles both in the newsroom and outside the media industry. What I'm going to do in this talk is to share some of our experiences and reflection from the first five years of the programme.

One thing we were sure of right from the beginning is that we wanted to move beyond just a tools based approach. Having a large set of tools that you can use as a journalist is great, but at the forward edges of any industry that is connected to technology, things move fast, and things break all the time, and this is often by design. During the time we've been running the programme we've seen any number of free tools for data collection, scraping or analysis pivot to a freemium or even premium model that leaves organisations struggling for funding out in the cold. This can be a significant challenge, and we want students that can get past these issues. Which is part of the reason for heading down the more coding intensive 'computational' path. Why 'computational' - why not just 'data journalism'. We want graduates who are capable beyond the simple applications of tools and processes to relatively small or easily handled data - we're looking more at graduates able to apply computational thinking processes (simplification, abstraction, modelling, solution building) within journalism - using larger and more complex data sets, unstructured data - problems that may sometimes require the building of new tools and processes to allow the story to be discovered and told. I'm not saying here that all journalists should learn to code. But as a computer scientist, I strongly believe that more people should understand the computational approach and the benefits it can bring

Of course, given that we're designing a course that is covering a very new area, there's no guidebook in play that could help us. The QAA in the UK does not have subject level benchmarks for Computational Journalism, and the professional accrediting bodies are somewhat behind the times when it comes even to fairly standard 'data' journalism, let alone anything more technical, and of course this can be understood when you consider that not all journalists striclty need these skills, no matter how much we would prefer that they have them Berret & Phillips, "Teaching Data and Computational Journalism", (2016) has four key areas of Data and Computational Journalism Teaching. You can easily get to the first two with some basic 'in-house' j-school coding, or even using simple tools-based training and teaching, and might stretch to the third, but the fourth is unreachable without a solid foundation of CS learning that you are not going to get without CS involvement in the course.
"Journalism schools should build collaborative partnerships with other disciplines. Many professional schools, journalism included, have tended to operate as silos within universities because they draw their culture and concerns from a field of practice rather than a tradition of academic discourse. That stance must shift because journalism itself is shifing. As a result, we should recognize that journalism is not a narrow set of traditional newsroom skills, but instead encompasses whatever tools and methods have, in one way or another, been made journalistic"

Our first challenge with this course is around access. This is not just a 'finishing school' for journalism graduates - we wanted this course to be open to as many people as possible, which means our intake has a very wide spread. We accept journalism graduates of course, alongside working journalists (many of whom take a career break to come and re-train with us), but we also take in more technical STEM graduates, who perhaps have coding experience and no journalistic knowledge, or even graduates from other disciplines who have neither coding nor journalistic background. This wide range of intakes presents many challenges, and I'll mention a few of the ways we have overcome these in a moment
So in the first semester our students study 20 credits of Journalism modules and 40 credits of Computer Science modules. To begin with, the Data Journalism module was actually a 'Digital Journalism' module, but as the Journalism School recognised the importance of Data skills (and because the 'Digital' skills being taught in the digital module had been largely subsumed into other modules), it was swapped out for the Data Journalism module. This gives the students a much better understanding of Data at an earlier stage of the programme. This starts from a tool based approach (used across all the PGT programmes in the School of Journalism), but also advances into R as the students gain coding knowledge on the Computer Science side. On this side, they are taught introductory programming modules alongside other students on 'conversion' degree programmes in the School. This provides a very useful learning experience which the students would not get if we were purely teaching coding within a Journalism programme: the students are able to see and hear from other students who are also approaching coding for the first time, and are able to understand that they are not necessarily special or unique just because of their background/intended career. 'Coding is hard for us as journalists' is a thing we have heard before, and being able to point at the other 90 students in the class and provide a counter-example is very helpful - look, here is someone with an english degree who is also finding this tough, and here is someone else who isn't - yes learning to code can be tricky, or it can be easy, and seeing the wide range of experiences is therefore helpful. It also allows a real connectivist approach to the learning - students can form learning networks with other students, improving their learning experiences. Of course, this is a trade-off, because it means that some of the coding examples they see early on are perhaps not specifically or easily applied to the journalistic context. This can be good, because it allows the student to understand the wider context and the larger general applicability of the techniques and skills they are learning, but can also alienate them slightly from the learning, if the direct applicability is not easily seen. One solution to this is the first semester 'harmonisation seminars' we run.
The Lab 'harmonisation seminars' are an space within the programme with no formal assessment that allow us to do two things: 1. they allow us to help get everyone up to the same level in the wide range of skills - so we can support those students needing help with coding more, or those students needing help with writing more. 2. They allow us to start to apply the skills they are learning in other modules on side projects, and show how the two things relate together. Within this space we can provide a safe space for the students to explore, experiment, and really importantly fail with no consequences. We can use this space to begin side projects (that may evolve later in the second semester to larger scale projects) and to provide the students with opportunities to begin creating their portfolio of projects (useful for employment later)

In the second semester, the students choose from a wide range of optional modules - on the Computer Science side they can pick things such as Data Visualisation, or Human Centric Computing. On the Journalism side there are academic modules looking at Business Journalism, managing media, crisis reporting and so on. The key part of the second semester is a team investigative project lasting all semester, where students work in groups to create 'something' - somehwere on a scale from a 'traditional' data investigation and reporting, to a newsroom tool to aid investigations/reporting. Authentic learning experience - close to real world (as close a 'simulation' as we can get). In this project, the students pitch ideas to us and the rest of the class, and then work on projects for 11 weeks, until final delivery at the end of the semester. This module has been singled out by our external examiner, (an editor at a national news organisation in the UK) as being an exemplary experience for the students, and replicating real world situations in the newsroom as far as possible. Examples of the types of projects that students complete in this module have included a tool that links trending news articles on news websites to emoji use on Twitter, a large data investigation into the effect of austerity in the UK on educational funding, and a large-scale analysis of Instagram hashtags connected to self-harm. This module often suffers from the usual problems that are found in group modules, and sometimes the teams need a careful watch to ensure that the group dynamics do not shift too much and cause the team to tip into a crisis. We've noticed this sometimes threatens particularly when combining experienced working journalists with younger less-experienced students, or when attempting to bring people with a very individualistic approach into a team project

The ultimate proof of whether the programme works or not is of course down to the outcomes of the students, and we think this is where we can show a definite success. Many of our former students are now working in newsrooms around the globe, in roles ranging from newsroom developer, through visualisation producer, and into more 'traditional' data journalist roles. Our graduates are working for The Guardian, The Times and the Sunday Times, The Bureau Local (part of the TBIJ), NZZ in Switzerland, and Data Journalism outfits in India. We also have graduates working outside of traditional news media in news type roles, such as for the Office of National Statistics in the UK. And then of course we have a range of students who have gone on to non-media or news roles, working in PR or consultancy, which demonstrates the transferability and wide applicability of the skills covered in the course.
  1. Immerse the students in both CS and Journalism
  2. Close the gap - make it relevant
  3. Give students space to explore, experiment and fail
  4. Be authentic
